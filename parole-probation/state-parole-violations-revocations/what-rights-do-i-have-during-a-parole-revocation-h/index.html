What rights do I have during a parole revocation hearing?

The superior court judge cannot suspend or revoke your parole unless there is ‚Äúgood cause‚Äù (a good reason) to believe that you have violated your parole conditions.[564]

As a parolee, you have the following rights:

The right to receive written notice of the alleged violation (the rule or law that Parole claims you have broken) and possible consequences, with enough information to allow you to prepare a defense and obtain evidence that can help explain or justify your actions.[565]

The right to have Parole disclose any evidence against you (just like at trial).[566]

The right to timely hearing of the charges ‚Äî including a probable cause hearing and a formal revocation hearing (within 90 days if you were not granted bail).[567]

The right to present witnesses and documentary evidence.[568]

The right to confront and cross-examine witnesses who speak against your interest[569] You have a conditional right under the U.S. and California constitutions to cross-examine people whose statements are used against you in a parole violation proceeding.[570] BUT SEE NOTE BELOW!

The right to a ‚Äúneutral and detached hearing body‚Äù (meaning a fair and unbiased judge).[571]

The right to receive a written statement of the decision, the evidence relied on, and the reasons for revoking parole.[572]

IMPORTANT! You may waive (give up) your right to a revocation hearing by admitting that you violated parole.[573] If you believe your rights are violated during the process or in a hearing, you should SAY SOMETHING. If you are unsure about whether your rights have been violated, or if you don‚Äôt understand your rights, ask your lawyer or public defender. If you do not have a lawyer or public defender, call your local public defender‚Äôs office.

IMPORTANT NOTE ABOUT WITNESSES: Witnesses may not be required to testify in front of you at your parole revocation hearing if they are considered to be fearful or confidential.[574] Before your hearing, talk with your lawyer about all potential witnesses ‚Äî those for you and those against you.

‚ÄúFearful witnesses‚Äù are witnesses whose identity is known to you but who: (1) have indicated that they are at a risk of harm if they testify in your presence; or (2) have requested that their contact information be withheld from you. ‚ÄúConfidential witnesses‚Äù are witnesses, whose identity is not known to you, and who would be at a risk of harm if their identity were disclosed.[575] However, if confidential information is used as part of the evidence that you violated parole, you can ask the District Attorney (prosecutor) to show you this information.
